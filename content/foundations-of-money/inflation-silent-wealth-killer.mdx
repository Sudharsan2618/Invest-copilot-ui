## Why This Article Will Change How You See Your Savings Account

Right now, you probably have money sitting in a savings account. Maybe ₹50,000, maybe ₹5,00,000. It feels safe. The number only goes up. But here's what your bank doesn't tell you: **that money is losing value every single day.**

This isn't theory. This is arithmetic. And once you understand it, you'll never look at your bank balance the same way again.

<Analogy>
Imagine you're carrying a block of ice on a hot day. Every minute, it melts a little — so slowly you barely notice. After an hour, you're holding half the ice. After two hours, it's mostly water. You didn't drop it. You didn't lose it. It just... disappeared.

Your money in a savings account is that ice block. Inflation is the heat. The melting is invisible, but real. The difference? With ice, you can see the puddle. With money, you only realize the damage years later when you try to buy something.

</Analogy>

## What Inflation Actually Is (And Why the Official Number Lies to You)

Inflation isn't one number. It's a **personal** number that depends on what YOU spend money on.

The government publishes the <JargonTooltip term="CPI" definition="Consumer Price Index — a weighted average of prices for a basket of goods and services. Used as the official measure of inflation.">CPI</JargonTooltip> (Consumer Price Index), which averages prices across everything — from rice to refrigerators. But your life isn't average.

<KeyConcept title="Why This Matters for YOUR Investments">
If your **personal inflation** is 8-12% (which it likely is), then:

- A **savings account at 3.5%** = losing 4.5-8.5% per year in real terms
- A **Fixed Deposit at 7%** (5.6% after 20% tax) = losing 2.4-6.4% per year in real terms
- An **Index Fund at 12%** (10.8% after LTCG tax) = gaining 0-2.8% in real terms
- An **actively managed fund at 15%** = gaining 3-7% in real terms

**Only equity investments reliably beat personal inflation.** Everything else is slowly drowning.

</KeyConcept>

Read that last bar. **₹10 lakh today = ₹1.31 lakh of purchasing power in 30 years.** If you're 25 years old planning for retirement at 55, the money you're "saving" today needs to survive 30 years of inflation before you even use it.

This is why retirement planning isn't about "saving enough." It's about **investing enough to outrun inflation for decades.**

<IndianContext>
**Real numbers from India:**
- A basic engineering degree cost ₹50,000/year in 2000. Today it costs ₹2-5 lakh/year. That's 7-10x in 25 years (~10% annual inflation in education)
- A 1 BHK rent in Bangalore Whitefield was ₹5,000/month in 2010. Today it's ₹18,000-22,000. That's 3.5-4.5x in 15 years (~9% annual rent inflation)
- A plate of dosa & coffee in a restaurant was ₹35 in 2005. Today it's ₹120-150. That's 3.5-4.3x in 20 years (~7% food inflation)

**The pattern:** Everything you NEED (housing, education, healthcare, food) inflates faster than things you WANT (phones, gadgets, clothes). This means inflation hits harder if you're not wealthy.

</IndianContext>

## Why "Safe" Investments Are Actually Dangerous

Here's the cruel irony: the investments marketed as "safe" — savings accounts, FDs, recurring deposits — are the **most dangerous** for long-term wealth. They don't lose your principal (the number stays), but they silently destroy your purchasing power (what the number can buy).

<CaseStudy title="Your Parents' FD Strategy — Did It Work?">
In 1995, your parents might have put ₹1,00,000 in a 10% FD (rates were higher then). By 2025, with interest reinvested, that became approximately ₹17.4 lakh. Sounds great!

But let's check: if they had invested in the BSE Sensex (which went from 3,000 to 75,000 in the same period), that ₹1 lakh would be **₹25 lakh**. And if they had invested in a diversified equity fund averaging 15%, it would be **₹66 lakh**.

The FD kept their money "safe" but cost them **₹49 lakh in opportunity cost.** Safety has a price, and that price is steep over decades.

</CaseStudy>

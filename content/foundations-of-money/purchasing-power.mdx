## The Number in Your Account is Lying to You

Your bank balance says ₹3,00,000. It said ₹2,50,000 last year. You're ₹50,000 richer, right?

**Wrong.** If prices rose 8% this year, you needed ₹2,70,000 just to maintain the same lifestyle. Your ₹50,000 gain looks good on paper, but only ₹30,000 of it is _real_ improvement. The other ₹20,000 is just keeping up with rising prices.

This gap between what your money _says_ and what it can _do_ is called <JargonTooltip term="Purchasing Power" definition="The real quantity of goods and services your money can buy. As prices rise, purchasing power falls — even if your bank balance stays the same.">purchasing power</JargonTooltip>.

<Analogy>
Imagine money is like a ruler, but the ruler keeps shrinking. Today, your ruler is 12 inches. Next year, it's 11.3 inches. The year after, 10.6 inches. The table you're measuring hasn't changed — but your ruler says it's getting bigger every year.

That's exactly what happens with money. The "ruler" (₹1) measures less and less over time. So when something costs ₹200 instead of ₹150, the item didn't get "more expensive" — your measuring tool got smaller.

</Analogy>

<KeyConcept title="The Pattern You Must See">
Notice something? **Things you NEED** (housing, education, healthcare, food) inflate at 8-12%. **Things you WANT** (phones, gadgets, cars) inflate at 0-3% or even decrease.

This creates a cruel paradox: **the poorer you are, the more inflation hurts you**, because a larger share of your income goes to necessities. And **the more you earn, the easier it is to beat inflation**, because you have surplus to invest.

This is why learning to invest isn't optional — it's the only escape from this treadmill.

</KeyConcept>

## The Money Illusion: Why Raises Don't Make You Richer

Economists call this the <JargonTooltip term="Money Illusion" definition="The tendency to think about money in nominal terms (the number) rather than real terms (what it can buy). A 5% raise feels good even if inflation is 7%.">money illusion</JargonTooltip> — and it tricks almost everyone.

After 5 years, your salary went from ₹40K to ₹50.5K — a 26% increase! You celebrated each raise. But adjusted for inflation, **your purchasing power barely changed** — and in some years, it **decreased**. You ran faster but the treadmill sped up too.

<IndianContext>
**The average Indian salary increase is 8-10% in IT and 5-7% in other sectors.** But personal inflation for urban professionals is 7-10%. This means:

- **IT professionals:** Barely staying ahead of inflation (1-3% real gains)
- **Government employees:** Often falling behind inflation (DA adjustments are slow)
- **Startup employees:** Variable — but ESOPs are only valuable if the company succeeds

**The takeaway:** Salary raises alone will NOT make you wealthy. You need your money to work harder than you do. That's what investing is for.

</IndianContext>

### The Retirement Trap

Most Indians who say "I need ₹2 crore to retire" are using today's numbers. At 7% inflation, maintaining the same lifestyle in 25 years requires **₹13.7 crore.** That's not a typo — 6.8x more than what they're planning for.

This is why so many retired Indians struggle financially even though they "saved enough" when they were working. They planned for today's prices but retired into tomorrow's prices.

</ConceptImage>

When cricket happens in India, it's usually at Wankhede, Eden Gardens, or Chinnaswamy. Different stadiums, same game. India's stock exchanges work the same way â€” different venues, same stocks, same purpose.

But understanding the differences helps you make sense of the financial news you'll encounter every day.

<Analogy>
  Think of NSE and BSE like Amazon and Flipkart. Both sell many of the same
  products (stocks). Both are regulated by the same government body (SEBI, like
  consumer protection laws). Prices are nearly identical because of competition.
  You can buy from either one â€” the product you receive (shares in your Demat
  account) is exactly the same regardless of which platform you used.
</Analogy>

<IndianContext>
  Most Indian investors interact primarily with NSE because it has higher
  trading volumes (meaning better liquidity and tighter bid-ask spreads).
  However, BSE is important too â€” many companies are listed only on BSE, and
  BSE's SME platform lists smaller companies that might become tomorrow's blue
  chips. When people say "the market went up 500 points," they usually mean
  Sensex (BSE) went up 500 points, since Sensex is the older, more recognized
  index in mainstream media.
</IndianContext>

## Understanding Market Indices: Nifty 50 and Sensex

An <JargonTooltip term="Index" definition="A basket of selected stocks that represents the overall market or a segment of it. Changes in the index reflect the average performance of those stocks.">index</JargonTooltip> is like a class average. Instead of checking every student's score, you check the class average to understand overall performance. Nifty 50 is the "class average" of India's 50 biggest companies.

<KeyConcept title="What the Nifty 50 Actually Is">
  Nifty 50 picks the 50 largest and most liquid companies across sectors â€”
  Reliance, TCS, HDFC Bank, Infosys, ITC, and 45 others. Each company has a
  different "weight" based on its market cap. When Reliance goes up 2%, it moves
  Nifty more than when a smaller company goes up 2%, because Reliance has a
  bigger weight.
</KeyConcept>

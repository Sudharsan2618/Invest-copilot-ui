Let's play a game. Imagine it's a normal Tuesday. You're at work. Your phone rings. Your parent has been hospitalised. The hospital wants ₹2,00,000 as an admission deposit. Right now. Today.

Do you have it? Without selling investments? Without calling friends? Without using a credit card at 42% interest?

If the answer is no, you're not ready to invest. You're not ready for financial goals. You need this first.

<Analogy>
  An emergency fund is like the foundation of a house. It's underground — nobody
  sees it, nobody talks about it at parties, it doesn't look impressive. But
  without it, the entire house collapses the moment the ground shakes. Your
  investments, your goals, your financial plan — they all sit on top of this
  boring, invisible foundation.
</Analogy>

## Why Most Indians Don't Have One (And Pay the Price)

<IndianContext>
  According to a 2023 survey by BankBazaar, **only 27% of Indian salaried
  professionals have an emergency fund** that covers 3+ months of expenses. The
  remaining 73% are one unexpected event away from financial crisis. The most
  common emergencies in India: medical hospitalisations (avg cost ₹1-5 lakhs
  without insurance), job loss (avg time to find new job: 2-4 months), vehicle
  breakdown/accident (₹30,000-1,50,000), family emergency requiring urgent
  travel.
</IndianContext>

<KeyConcept title="Who Needs 3 Months vs 6 Months?">
  **3 months is enough if:** You have a stable job, health insurance, dual income household, and no dependents.

**Target 6 months if:** You're the sole earner, have dependents (parents, spouse, children), work in an unstable industry (startups, freelancing), or have a pre-existing medical condition.

</KeyConcept>

<CaseStudy title="Sneha's ₹3,00,000 Hospital Bill">
  **Sneha**, a 29-year-old HR manager in Bangalore, earned ₹65,000/month. She had been investing ₹15,000/month in mutual funds for 2 years — her portfolio was worth ₹4,20,000. But she had no emergency fund.

When her father needed emergency heart surgery, the hospital demanded ₹3,00,000 upfront. She had to redeem her mutual funds — which were down 12% at that time due to a market correction. She got back only ₹3,70,000 instead of ₹4,20,000.

**She lost ₹50,000** because she was forced to sell at the wrong time. If she'd had a ₹2,00,000 emergency fund in a liquid fund + savings account, she would have used that and let her investments recover. Her mutual funds are now worth ₹5,10,000 — she missed ₹1,40,000 in gains.

**Total cost of not having an emergency fund: ₹1,90,000.**

</CaseStudy>

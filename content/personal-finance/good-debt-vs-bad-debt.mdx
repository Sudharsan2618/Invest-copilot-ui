"Debt is bad." You've heard this your whole life. Your parents probably told you to never borrow money. Many Indian families treat debt like a moral failure.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **that advice is wrong.** Some debt makes you richer. Some debt makes you poorer. And knowing the difference is one of the most important financial skills you'll ever learn.

<Analogy>
  Debt is like fire. In a kitchen, fire cooks your food and keeps you alive. In
  your bedroom, fire burns your house down. The fire itself isn't good or bad â€”
  it's WHERE and HOW you use it. Same with debt. An education loan "cooks" your
  career. Credit card debt "burns" your finances.
</Analogy>

## The Debt Payoff Order (The "Avalanche" Method)

If you have multiple debts, here's the mathematically optimal payoff order:

1. **Credit card debt** (36-42%) â€” this is a CODE RED emergency
2. **Personal loans** (14-18%) â€” pay aggressively
3. **Car loan** (9-12%) â€” regular EMIs are fine
4. **Education loan** (8-10%) â€” keep paying EMIs, invest alongside
5. **Home loan** (7-9%) â€” lowest priority, has tax benefits

  **Meera**, 27, an architect in Mumbai earning â‚¹55,000/month, had accumulated:

- Credit card balance: â‚¹80,000 at 40% APR (min payment â‚¹4,000/month â€” mostly interest)
- Personal loan: â‚¹2,00,000 at 15% (EMI â‚¹9,400/month)
- Education loan: â‚¹4,00,000 at 8.5% (EMI â‚¹8,200/month)

She was also investing â‚¹5,000/month in mutual funds. Sounds responsible? Let's do the math:

- Mutual fund expected return: **12%**
- Credit card interest she's paying: **40%**
- Net result: She's **losing 28% per year** on every rupee she invests while carrying credit card debt

**What she should have done:** Stop the â‚¹5,000 SIP temporarily, add it to credit card payments. Clear the â‚¹80,000 credit card in 6-7 months. Then attack the personal loan. Then restart SIPs â€” now without the 40% anchor dragging her down.

**Cost of the mistake:** By keeping the credit card debt for an extra year while investing, Meera lost approximately â‚¹22,000 in net interest.

</CaseStudy>

<KeyConcept title="The Math is Simple">
  If you're paying 15%+ interest on any debt while investing for 12% returns,
  **your investments are going backwards**. Pay off high-interest debt first.
  It's the guaranteed, risk-free "investment" with the highest return available
  to you.
</KeyConcept>

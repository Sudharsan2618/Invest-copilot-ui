You've decided to invest. Congratulations. But invest in WHAT?

Open any investment app and you're bombarded: stocks, mutual funds, bonds, gold, FDs, PPF, NPS, ETFs, REITs, SGBs, government securities... It's overwhelming. No wonder most people just default to FDs and forget about it.

Here's the good news: **all of these fit into just 5 categories.** Learn these 5 categories and the entire investment universe makes sense.

<Analogy>
  Think of asset classes like food groups. You have grains (carbs), protein,
  vegetables, fruits, and fats. Each serves a different purpose in your body. No
  single food group can keep you healthy alone — you need a balanced diet.
  Similarly, each asset class serves a different purpose in your portfolio.
  Stocks are your protein (growth). Bonds are your carbs (stability). Gold is
  your vitamins (insurance). Cash is water (always needed). The right mix keeps
  your financial health strong.
</Analogy>

<KeyConcept title="The Core Principle">
  When you invest in **equity**, you're an OWNER. When you invest in **debt**,
  you're a LENDER. When you buy **gold**, you're a HOARDER (storing value). When
  you buy **real estate**, you're a LANDLORD. When you hold **cash**, you're a
  SAVER. Each role pays differently — owners get the highest reward because they
  take the highest risk.
</KeyConcept>

<CaseStudy title="Rahul's Concentrated Bet vs Priya's Diversification">
  **Rahul** was convinced equities were the best asset class (true over 20 years!). He put 100% of his ₹5,00,000 savings in Nifty 50 in January 2008.

**By December 2008:** His portfolio crashed to ₹2,10,000. A 58% loss. He panicked and sold — locking in the loss permanently.

**Priya** split her ₹5,00,000: 60% equity (₹3,00,000), 20% gold (₹1,00,000), 20% debt (₹1,00,000).

**By December 2008:** Equity portion: ₹1,26,000 (-58%). Gold portion: ₹1,25,000 (+25%). Debt portion: ₹1,08,000 (+8%). **Total: ₹3,59,000** — a 28% loss instead of 58%.

The 30% less damage meant Priya didn't panic. She held. By 2010, her portfolio recovered to ₹6,20,000. Rahul, who sold in panic, still had ₹2,10,000 in his savings account.

**Same starting money. Same market. Diversification saved Priya ₹4,10,000.**

</CaseStudy>
